THE NBA: Part 2 - The History.

The NBA is 75 years old. Thats pretty young in terms of sports league ages. Even Basketball as a whole is a pretty young sport compared to many of its contemporaries.

For example: Hockey dates back thousands of years, Baseball is 200+ years old, American Football is over 150 years old and soccer is ancient.

To even further show how young Basketball is, the NHL was founded in 1917, the MLB was founded in 1876, the NFL was founded in 1920 and the first soccer world cup was in 1930.

    Each position's role has morphed and mutated over the years to the point where now a power forward can play like a point guard and nobody bats an eye.

    Still though, the roles were much more concrete back when the NBA was in its infant stages and thats what this section will talk about.

    The History of the Point Guard

    1950s: The Point Guard position only had one true goal. That goal was to pass the ball. A great example of a super traditional point guard was Slater Martin the man that was the point guard for the Lakers.

    The Lakers star George Mikan was getting most of his point from Martin passing to him. This helped the lakers win a lot of championships in the 50s.

    The 50s also saw the introduction of the first true legendary point guard: Bob Cousy.

    Bob Cousy was touted as the first player to dazzle the NBA. He pioneered a lot of what many point guards do today like shooting from a distance, having handles and being flashy.

    1960s: With the arrival of Bill Russel to Cousy's Celtics the modern fastbreak was invented.

    Cousy would grab the ball off a missed shot and bring it down the court to pass to Russell who scored with a dunk or a layup on the inside.

    Cousy fully retired at the start of an era he kickstarted.

    1970s: The 70s saw yet another evolution of how the point guard played with the careers of two NBA greats: Lenny Wilkens and Walt Frazier.

    While Wilkens was at the tail end of his career in the 70s, he was one of the first point guards to truly be the leader of a team and to have great defense.

    He also brought a type of confidence that nobody had saw before.

    Walt Frazier was also a player much like Wilkens. He led his Knicks to two titles in the 70s and was the textbook example of a leader on court.

    1980s: The 80s brought the biggest change yet. While Cousy was flashy nothing could compare to someone who was magical.

    Magic Johnson is widely considered the greatest point guard of all time and its hard to see a reason to argue against that.

    He brought a sense of style and flashiness that nobody in the NBA had ever seen before. He passed, scored, rebounded and pretty much did everything

    Without Magic the point guard position would likely be stuck in its 70s roots.

    1990s and 2000s: These two decades didn't bring much revolution to the point guard position. Players like Gary Payton and Allen Iverson were good point guards but didn't do much to add to the position as a whole.

    2010s - now: Stephen Curry. He revoloutionized the point guard position a lot. Infact, he changed the NBA as a whole.

    Stephen Curry single handedly made many players but especially the guard positions to shoot a lot of threes and to mainly run the offense.

    Because of him guards like Luka Doncic and Trae Young are now thriving in the NBA.

    The History of the Shooting Guard

    1950s - 1980s : The shooting guard is a position that hasn't went through much changes.

    Old shooting guards of the 50s and 60s like Bill Sharman, Jerry West and Oscar Robertson were the main offensive threat for the teams that they played for.

    They only really shot from mid range and close range and were known to get insane amounts of points.

    In the 70s and 80s shooting guards like Pete Maravich and Clyde Drexler were more flashy and atheltic than their predecessors but not by a drastic amount

    1990s: Then Came Michael Jordan. Widely thought of as the greatest NBA player of all time, he dominated the 1990s and became an inspiration for shooting guards and other positions alike for many years to come.

    Jordan was incredibly athletic, deadly on both sides of the court and was not a bad all around player as well.

    He made it so that more players would be like him rather than the players of old.

    2000s: One player to follow in his footsteps was Kobe Bryant. He played very similar to Jordan and furthered the idea to play like Jordan in the minds of many kids watching him.

    2010s - now. With Stephen Curry doing what he did, many Shooting Guards nowadays rely on the three point line as their weapon of choice.

    The History of the Small Forward.

    The Small Forward has probably gone through the most drastic changes out of the 5 positions.

    1950s: In the early stages of the small forward, it was considered to be the worst and most useless position. THere was no real point to having the small forward there on the court.

    In the 1960s and 1970s though, the likes of Elgin Baylor and Julius Erving brought the position to new heights. These forwards were bigger, stronger faster and were much better on defense.

    The 70s made it so that the small forward position was essentially a balance of the shooting guard and power forward position.

    The 80s further built upon that with the likes of Larry bird and Adrian Dantely coming into the freigh.

    These players made the small forward notable for scoring and also being the primary defender on their team.

    The 90s and 2000s saw many small forwards become incredibly strong on versatile with the likes of Rashard Lewis and Shawn Marion.

    Finally the 2010s and 2020s saw Kevin Durant and Lebron James being the best players in the league while also showcasing how perfectly balanced this position is.

    The History of the Power Forward.

    The power forward is much like the shooting guard. They both have not gone through much changes but are weirdly inbetween the positions that went through the most.

    In 50s and 60s power forwards ( and players as a whole ) were nowhere near today's players in terms of strenght, athletisicsm and skill.

    Power forwards like Bob Petit were not the most physically gifted but were still good enough to make a name for themselves in the NBA.

    As the 70s and 80s came the power forward position became somewhat of a secondary center position. Players like Moses Malone and Connie Hawkins weren't tall enough for the center position so they just played the next best thing.

    Larry Nance and Charles Oakley are also great examples of this.

    The 90s and 2000s brought somewhat of a rennaisance for the power forward position though.

    These decades saw the likes of Tim Duncan, Kevin Garnett and Dirk Nowitzki become legends of their sport and revoloutinize how the power forward is played.

    The 2010s furthered this notion as players like Pascal Siakam and Karl Anthony Towns became perfect representations of the power forwards of the decade before them.

    These players made it so that the position wasn't really just the second center, but rather it was a position that was a blend of Small forward and the center position as a whole.

    The history of the Center.

    The Center position has gone through the weirdest changes throughout the course of the nba.

    1950 - 1990. The Center position didn't really go through much changes in the first 40 years of the NBA.

    The players in this position were often the guy who played the most defense and rebounded the most. They were also often the biggest and strongest guy on the court.

    The likes of George Mikan, Wilt Chamberlain, Bill Russel, Kareem Abdul Jabbar and Dave Cowens are all examples of how centers were for 40 years.

    Then the 90s came. The Center position became more dominant and mobile as a whole. Players like Shaquille O'Neal, Hakeem Olajuwon and David Robinson were all much more athletic and dominant than most of their predesscesors.

    These players all won championships in the 90s and early 2000s.

    The 2000s and early 2010s saw what was described to be the dark ages of the center. Not many centers were really comparable to the likes of the ones of the past and the center slowly became the position with the least attraction.

    The centers that were popular though like Dwight Howard and Ben Wallace were pretty similar to the likes of the 90s centers.

    After Curry revoloutionized the NBA the center position was revitalized.

    Players like Nikola Jokic, Joel Embiid and Rudy Gobert showed that a center didn't have to be a one trick pony and rather they could be the most versatile position in the league.

    Logo History

    Atlanta Hawks

    The Hawks have gone through a decent bit of logo changes throughout their history. Their first logo was completely different from the rest of the Hawks logos to follow and even now the designs have become more minmalistic.

    Boston Celtics

    The Celtics haven't gone through many logo changes as of recent. Each of them have been mainly centered on the theme of luck and almost all feature Lucky the Leprechaun.

    Brooklyn Nets

    Most of the Nets logos have followed the same few themes. Red, White and Blue colour scheme, and always to have a basketball in the logo. Their most recent logo though signalled a rebrand for the team.

    Charlotte Hornets

    Most of the Hornets/Bobcats logos have followed the central theme of their namesake. Infact they haven't really gone through much logo changes throughout their history

    Chicago Bulls

    The Chicago Bulls have the luxury of getting their logo right the first time around.

    They have not changed their logo since the 1960s.

    Cleveland Caveliers

    The Cavs have always had pretty drastic logo changes but they almost always focused at least a little on the sword aspect of the Cavaliers name.

    Dallas Mavericks

    The Mavericks have only really had two logos ever. They've also pretty much stuck with the cowboy aspect of their name.

    The Denver Nuggets have almost always kept up their logo theme as something indicative of where they were established: Colorado.

    Detroit Pistons

    The pistons have had an interesting logo history. For a few years the logo was a tin man and for a few it was a horse, but most of the time its the basic circle with the name of the team with varying colours.

    Golden State Warriors

    The Warriors have almost always had a logo that showed their team colours, whether it was the blue of yellow. Most of their logos have also shown the Golden Gate Bridge as well.

    Houston Rockets

    The Rockets logos have always shown a small part of it that has to do with their namesake. Even the new ones have very tiny details at the bottom of the R.

    Indiana Pacers

    The Pacers have always kept their central "P" in their logo.

    Los Angeles Clippers

    The Clippers have had a lot of drastic logo changes throughout their years.

    Los Angeles Lakers

    Most of the Lakers logos have been centered around the basketball. Even the very first one when they were back in Minneapolis showed the basketball at the center of the logo.

    Memphis Grizzlies

    The Grizzlies have always shown their namesake in their logos but over the years it has gotten more simplistic.

    Miami Heat

    The Heat have had only 2 logos in their history and its both been flaming basketballs

    Milwaukee Bucks

    The Bucks are yet another team that have always had their logos focused on their namesake Buck.

    Minnesota Timberwolves

    The Timberwolves are one of those teams that have always made their logos become less and less detailed over time.

    New Orleans Pelicans

    Since the Pelicans history intersects with the Hornets history, their starting logo and most of their logos have been centered around the Hornet.

    New York Knicks

    The Knicks have almost always been a team that has kept a basketball in their logo. It has also always been orange

    Oklahoma City Thunder

    Okc hasn't always been the Thunder. Before they were the Thunder, they were the SuperSonics. Most of their logos have shown something that made it known that they were from Seattle.

    Orlando Magic

    The Magic have only had 3 logos. Each of them have been the name of the team and a magical ball strewn across the screen

    Philadelphia 76ers

    One thing that is always consistent with the 76ers logos is that 76 will always be front and center.

    Phoenix Suns

    The Suns have always had the same box with the flaming basketball logo but the colours haven't always been the same.

    Portland Trail Blazers

    The Blazers are yet another team that has kept the same logo design for its entire duration but with small changes.

    Sacremento Kings

    For the most part the Kings kept the Basketball combined with a crown design just with different team names or colours.

    San Antonio Spurs

    The Spurs are a team that has almost always kept the same logo design except for a few color or design changes every now and then.

    Toronto Raptors

    The Raptors logos at first kept the Raptor as the main part of the design but as time went on they opted for a simplistic basketball that still showed the raptor influence.

    Utah Jazz

    The Utah Jazz have had some pretty drastic logo changes over the years. they've had mountains to literally just their name at points.
